A Mixed Reality Empowerment for Hearing-Loss Individuals

Overview

Empower Hearing-Loss Individuals with Mixed Reality

Bubble Talk is a mixed reality application that empowers hearing loss individuals to navigate daily conversations with greater ease and confidence. It visualizes verbal messages in an informative, interactive, and responsive manner, allowing users to effortlessly comprehend information, smoothly extend conversation, and easily adapt various conditions.

Research

We researched the current state of hearing loss from a global, societal, and individual perspective, evaluated existing solutions and interviewed people with hearing loss to gain a in-depth understanding of the conversational difficulties they experience.

Background Research

How has hearing loss impacted individuals on different levels?

We researched papers and data on health and well-being of hearing loss individuals on global, societal, and individual level to understand their state of being.

Hearing loss affects people of all ages and is prevalent in every country. An estimated 20% of the global population live with hearing loss in 2022 according to WHO.

Global Scale

Hearing loss can impact an individual's ability to participate fully in society. Fewer educational and job opportunities are given to hearing-loss individuals due to impaired communication.

Societal State

Hearing loss can affect a person's ability to communicate and and participate in social and recreational activities, which can lead to feelings of isolation, loneliness and frustration.

Individual Level

What are the existed solutions for hearing-loss individuals to enhance communications?

We evaluated various hardware and software utilized by hearing loss individuals to enhance day-to-day communication, accessing their pros and cons.

How would mixed-reality application excel?

Mixed-Reality Glasses + Real-Time Caption

Mixed-reality glasses, as a head-mounted display, provide a virtual screen that moves with the user, making it an ideal solution for displaying information without disrupting daily activities. With real-time captioning, individuals with hearing loss can easily participate in conversations by seeing sentences.

3
Prototyping

Initially, a low-fidelity prototype was created for an first round retrospective review. Subsequently, we progressed to a mid-fidelity design to obtain more user testing to decide on the final features and design that would be delivered.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

Our low-fidelity prototype included the basic user interface and visual elements for the app's core functionality, such as text bubble conversation, conversation history, and sound notifications.

These features were designed with various usage scenarios in mind, such as conversations with individuals out of view or group conversations.

Mid-Fidelity Prototype

In our mid-fidelity prototype, we modified the display logic of speech bubbles, moving it to the bottom of users’ view zone. We also decided on the operational logic of the software's system setting, that it will be displayed and made available to the users on their left hand palm.

In this phase of our prototyping, we finalized majority of our design decisions, leaving space for more detailed developments in our final high-fidelity prototype which is presented in the form of a demonstration video.

Design Iterations

Control Panel Design Interactions Based on User Feedback

We iteratively designed our control panel based on user feedback obtained at various stages, which led us to the final design that is both easy and intuitive to control.

Menu Mid-Air

In the first iteration, setting menu was located mid-air with a wrap effect. Although this design is commonly used in nowadays VR applications, it breaks the immersion and requires excessive movements to control, which is less user-friendly than intended.

Two-Hand Control

We switched to a two-hand control panel design because users can intuitively hold up their hand to activate the control panel, and it also offers users a tangible interface to interact with. However, it still presents the issue of requiring excessive movement to make simple setting changes.

One-Hand Control

We ultimately decided on this one-hand control panel, which allows users to operate all settings with only pinch and move gestures on one hand. Although it raises the question of how to handle additional settings, we consider this to be an excellent design for the purpose of this product.
